How the 25 years unfold
YEARS 1–10
Bootstrap phase. One tiny, high-ROI market at a time — each one's annual profit funds the next country's capex outright. Only ~18 countries live by Year 10.
YEARS 11–22
Compounding takes over. Cash pool clears $2 Bn by Year 11 and the network hits its 12-new-country-per-year operational ceiling — the real constraint becomes aircraft delivery and regulatory bandwidth, not capital.
YEAR 23
All 169 profitable countries are live, generating a combined profit run-rate in the billions per year.
YEARS 24–25
The final 27 low-income countries — structurally loss-making — are brought online, cross-subsidised by the surplus from the other 169. Full global coverage: Year 25.

The perpetual model, after Year 25

No dividend is ever paid out. Profit only ever does one of two things.

1 · Build

Until Year 25, every dollar of profit goes into launching the next country — new fleet, lease deposits, ground infrastructure, working capital.

2 · Operate, perpetually

From Year 25 onward, with all 196 countries live, the ~$6.79 Bn/year profit run-rate recycles permanently into fleet renewal, rising load factors, deeper frequency, and absorbing the ongoing losses of the 27 low-income markets, forever.

Basis: merges the income-tier per-seat economics (fare/cost scaling by GNI^0.55 / GNI^0.35, anchored to India's ₹12,500 round trip) with the country-wise capex model (tier-scaled fixed licensing/IT/branding cost + variable lease-deposit/infra/working-capital cost × route count, route count bucketed by population from Micro/3 routes to Giant/130 routes). Annual profit = per-seat margin × passengers/year at 85% mature load factor. The reinvestment simulation greedily launches the next affordable country with the best profit-to-capex ratio each year, capped at 12 launches/year for operational realism. This is a planning-level extrapolation from a parametric model, not verified national-level costings or an audited multi-year projection.
