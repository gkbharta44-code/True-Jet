Bharat Wings — the 169 countries that are actually launchable today.

The 27 low-income countries flagged earlier aren't excluded on economics alone — many sit in active conflict, have no functioning aviation regulator, or lack basic fuel/ATC/rescue-and-fire infrastructure that no airline capex line can fix. This report re-runs the entire model on the remaining 169 countries that are genuinely operable now: capital ask, fleet, opex, launch timeline, and steady-state net profit — funded entirely by reinvesting profit, never distributed.

The 27 excluded — not on this timeline at all

Held out of the launch sequence entirely, not deferred to "later" — re-evaluated only as conditions change.

Active conflict / state fragility — no level of capital makes daily scheduled ops responsible right now. Fuel / ATC / RFF infrastructure gap — fixable, but by aid agencies and development banks before an airline flies there, not by airline capex. Closed political system — no market access for a foreign or private operator, infrastructure aside.

Where it goes after Year 23

With all 169 launchable countries live, there's no new country left to fund. The ~$7 Bn/year surplus recycles into fleet renewal (ATR airframes run ~20–25 years), added frequency and load-factor growth in existing markets, and a standing reserve that can fund entry into any of the 27 excluded countries the moment its conflict, regulatory, or infrastructure status changes.

What changed from the 196-country model

Total capex fell from $6.94Bn to $6.49Bn, timeline compressed from 25 to 23 years, and steady-state profit rose from $6.79Bn to $6.97Bn/yr — dropping 27 structurally loss-making, largely non-operable countries makes the network both faster to build and more profitable, not less.

Basis: same income-tier per-seat model as before (fare/cost scaling GNI^0.55/GNI^0.35, anchored to India's ₹12,500 round trip), restricted to the 169 high/upper-middle/lower-middle income countries. The 27 excluded low-income countries were removed on operational grounds (active conflict, absent fuel/ATC/rescue-and-fire infrastructure, or closed political systems), not purely financial ones — see Excluded 27. Fleet = ceil(routes/2) flying aircraft + 18% maintenance/AOG spares. The reinvestment simulation greedily launches the next affordable country with the best profit-to-capex ratio each year, capped at 12 launches/year for operational realism. This remains a planning-level parametric extrapolation, not verified national-level costings or an audited multi-year projection. Year-wise enterprise valuation applies an assumed EV/EBITDA multiple schedule (8.0x rising to a 16.0x growth-stage peak, compressing back to 8.3x — the current global airline industry trailing-12-month average per public market data — by Year 23) to each year's profit run-rate; this is an illustrative valuation range calibrated to real industry benchmarks, not a market-tested valuation for this specific unlisted entity.
